Rutherford's Nuclear Model

The model that revolutionized atomic structure by introducing the concept of a dense, positively charged nucleus.

Introduction

Rutherford's nuclear model, proposed in 1911, was a result of the Gold Foil Experiment. It suggested that atoms have a small, dense nucleus surrounded by orbiting electrons.

Structure of the Nucleus

The nucleus, located at the center of the atom, contains positively charged protons. The electrons move around the nucleus in relatively vast empty spaces.

Impact and Legacy

Rutherford’s model replaced the "plum pudding model" and paved the way for future atomic models, including Bohr’s model and quantum mechanical models of the atom.
